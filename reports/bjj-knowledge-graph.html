BJJ Knowledge Graph

Interactive knowledge graph mapping Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu positions, techniques, and transitions

TypeScript D3.js Vite Bun Biome Gi + No-Gi

bjj-knowledge-graph.vercel.app

The Challenge

BJJ has thousands of techniques, but no structured way to see how they connect.

Knowledge lives in scattered YouTube videos, instructional DVDs, and personal notebooks. There is no visual map of the art.

Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu is a positional martial art. Every move starts from a position and either leads to another position or ends the match. Closed Guard leads to Triangle Choke, which transitions to Mount or Armbar. Half Guard opens into Deep Half, Dogfight, or Back Take. These relationships form a massive directed graph, but practitioners have no tool to explore it.

Instructors teach techniques in isolation. Students memorize moves without understanding the web of transitions connecting them. The result: fragmented knowledge with no global picture of how positions relate to each other across the entire art.

Why It Matters

  • Learning path visualization — see which positions unlock which techniques. A beginner can trace the path from Closed Guard to Back Control and understand every transition along the way.
  • Technique discovery — click any position node to see all submissions, sweeps, escapes, and passes available from there. Find techniques you did not know existed from positions you already play.
  • Relationship mapping — understand that Guard Passing leads to Side Control which opens Mount, Knee on Belly, or Back Control. See the full attack chain before drilling it.
  • Gi vs No-Gi filtering — toggle between Gi, No-Gi, or Both modes. Spider Guard and Worm Guard disappear in No-Gi. Heel hooks light up in No-Gi. The graph adapts to your ruleset.

Key Insights

Force-Directed vs Hierarchical Layout

Force layout uses a physics simulation with charge repulsion, link attraction, cluster gravity, and collision detection. Positions naturally cluster by category (standing, top, bottom, leg locks). Good for exploring organic relationships.

Hierarchical layout arranges positions in layers by category from top to bottom. Better for understanding the positional hierarchy of BJJ: standing leads to top positions, which connect to bottom positions, which feed into leg lock entanglements.

Graph Data Model Design

Positions are nodes, not techniques. This is the key insight. A technique is not a standalone entity; it is a directed action from one position to another. An Armbar from Mount is a different thing than an Armbar from Guard, even though they share a name. By modeling techniques as edge metadata on transitions, the graph captures this naturally.

Technique Taxonomy

Six technique types map the entire art: submissions (76), sweeps (39), transitions (34), passes (17), takedowns (8), and escapes (7). Each type gets a distinct color on the graph edges, making it immediately visible whether a path is offensive (red submissions) or defensive (green escapes).

Cluster Force

A custom D3 force pulls nodes toward computed cluster centers based on their PositionCategory. Standing positions gravitate to the top, bottom guards cluster together, leg lock positions form their own island. The result is an organic layout that mirrors how practitioners mentally organize the art.

Gi/No-Gi as a First-Class Filter

Every position and technique carries gi and noGi boolean flags. Spider Guard, Lasso Guard, and Worm Guard are Gi-only. Rubber Guard is No-Gi-only. Toggling the filter dynamically shows or hides nodes and edges, revealing how the two rulesets produce different game graphs from the same underlying positions.
