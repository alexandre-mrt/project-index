Design of an Anonymous Credential System Leveraging Zero-Knowledge Proofs and Trusted Execution Environments for Identity Management and Low-Value Private Payments

EPFL MSc Thesis · Mysten Labs, Zurich

The Challenge

Digital identity systems force users to reveal their full identity for every interaction. Proving you are over 18 requires showing your name, address, and date of birth. Paying for a service links your real identity to transaction history. There is no selective disclosure, no unlinkability, no privacy.

Core tension: EU eIDAS 2.0 rejected ZKPs for the EUDI Wallet (mobile performance, protocol immaturity). The industry needs compliant privacy, but no formal framework exists for it.

Meanwhile, TEE-based solutions (Intel TDX, AMD SEV-SNP) are being broken with <$1000 hardware (TEE.Fail, TDXdown). Neither ZKP nor TEE alone is sufficient. A hybrid model is needed.

Why It Matters

  • Privacy as a right — users should prove attributes (age, residency, solvency) without revealing the underlying data
  • Regulatory alignment — selective disclosure satisfies KYC/AML without mass surveillance
  • Mysten Labs / Sui integration — builds on zkLogin (ACM CCS 2024) and Seal for a production-grade privacy layer
  • Bridging two worlds — ZKP provides cryptographic guarantees, TEE provides performance; combining them gives graceful degradation when either fails

    |                         |  "I am >18 AND        |                   |

    |                         |  (Pedersen commit,     |  (on-chain verify)  |
    |                         |   range proof)         |                   |

Hybrid model: TEE accelerates proof generation on mobile. If the TEE is compromised, the system falls back to pure ZKP — slower but cryptographically sound. Privacy never depends on hardware trust alone.

Key Insights

ZKP + TEE = graceful degradation. TEE compromise does not break privacy — the system falls back to pure ZKP. ZKP alone is too slow for mobile; TEE accelerates it. Neither is sufficient alone, but together they cover each other's weaknesses.

SAAC (CRYPTO 2025) is the closest related work: pairing-free, mobile-friendly anonymous credentials. Our construction extends this with TEE-assisted proving and Sui on-chain settlement.

Open problems: efficient revocation without breaking unlinkability (UPPR is promising but unproven at scale), and bridging the regulatory gap where eIDAS 2.0 demands selective disclosure but rejects the ZKP tooling that enables it.
